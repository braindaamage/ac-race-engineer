# Alignment

## Physical Principles

Wheel alignment describes the angular and spatial orientation of each wheel relative to the vehicle body and to each other. The three primary angles — camber, toe, and caster — each influence how the tyre's contact patch interacts with the road surface, and together they govern grip, stability, tyre wear, and steering response.

**Camber** is the inward or outward tilt of the wheel when viewed from the front of the car. A vertical wheel has zero camber. Negative camber (top of the wheel tilted inward) shifts the contact patch load toward the inner edge of the tyre. During cornering, body roll causes the outer tyre to lean toward positive camber relative to the road surface; applying static negative camber counteracts this effect and keeps a larger portion of the contact patch loaded through the corner. The trade-off is that excessive negative camber on a straight reduces the contact area and generates uneven heating, overworking the inner shoulder of the tyre. Optimal camber for each axle is the value that maximises contact patch utilisation during the predominant loading condition for that axle.

**Toe** refers to whether the front edges of the tyres point inward (toe-in) or outward (toe-out) relative to the direction of travel. Toe-in on the rear promotes straight-line stability by creating self-correcting yaw forces: if the car begins to rotate, the rear tyres generate opposing forces that resist the rotation. Toe-out on the front reduces the initial aligning torque the tyre must overcome before generating lateral force, sharpening turn-in response. Both conditions accelerate tyre wear because the tyre is constantly dragged at a small slip angle, scrubbing rubber from the contact patch. The magnitude of the wear is proportional to the absolute toe value.

**Caster** is the rearward tilt of the steering axis when viewed from the side of the car. A greater caster angle increases the mechanical trail — the horizontal distance between the steering axis contact point and the tyre contact patch centre. Larger mechanical trail increases the self-centering moment, improving straight-line stability and giving the driver stronger steering feel. Positive caster also causes the outer front tyre to gain negative camber during steering input (camber recovery through geometry), which improves cornering grip without requiring additional static camber. Higher caster increases steering effort and can amplify bump-steer sensitivity.

**Kingpin inclination (KPI)** and **scrub radius** are typically fixed by the suspension geometry. KPI is the inward tilt of the steering axis when viewed from the front; combined with caster it defines the steering axis in three dimensions. Scrub radius is the lateral distance at ground level between the steering axis contact point and the tyre centre. A small or negative scrub radius improves braking stability on split-friction surfaces (one side dry, one side wet) because braking forces create a self-correcting torque rather than a destabilising one.

**Adjustable vs Emergent Geometry in AC**
In Assetto Corsa, camber and toe are adjustable in the setup screen for virtually all cars. Caster is adjustable only on some cars — specifically those where a caster range is defined in the car's setup.ini file. Kingpin inclination (KPI), scrub radius, and Ackermann geometry are emergent properties of the suspension geometry defined by 3D attachment point coordinates in suspensions.ini (WBCAR_TOP_FRONT, WBCAR_TOP_REAR, WBTYRE_TOP, WBTYRE_BOTTOM, WBCAR_STEER, WBTYRE_STEER, and related points). These are editable only by modders who modify the suspension geometry files directly — they cannot be changed through the in-game setup screen. This distinction is important: discussions of caster, KPI, and scrub radius effects are informative for understanding car behavior but do not represent tuning options available to the user in AC.

**Dynamic Camber**
Static camber — the value set in the garage — is only the starting point. What determines cornering grip is the camber angle under cornering load, which differs from the static setting due to three dynamic contributors. First, geometry gain from the front view swing arm (FVSA): as the suspension compresses in roll, the wheel's camber changes based on the FVSA length — shorter swing arms produce more negative camber gain under compression, keeping the outer tyre more upright relative to the road. Second, caster-induced camber: positive caster causes the outer front wheel to gain negative camber when the steering is turned, providing additional cornering camber precisely when it is needed. This is a key reason why race cars use significant caster angles. Third, suspension travel dependent changes: the camber-vs-travel curve is non-linear and car-specific, determined by the complete suspension geometry. AC models all three contributors through its kinematic solver — the in-game developer suspension app can visualize the camber-vs-travel and camber-vs-steer curves for any car.

**Camber Thrust**
Camber thrust is the lateral force generated by a tyre purely due to its inclination angle, independent of any slip angle. When a tyre is tilted from vertical, the contact patch deforms asymmetrically, generating a net lateral force toward the direction of lean. For negative camber, this force pushes inward — toward the centre of the vehicle. During cornering, the heavily loaded outer wheel with negative camber generates camber thrust that supplements the slip-angle-generated lateral force. The magnitude of camber thrust is typically 10–20% of the slip-angle force at moderate camber angles. Typical camber reference values by vehicle category: street cars -0.5 to -1.5 degrees, GT and touring cars -2.0 to -3.5 degrees, open-wheel cars -2.5 to -4.0 degrees. Front axle camber is often set slightly more negative than the rear to compensate for the additional camber change caused by steering geometry (caster-induced positive camber on the inner wheel partially cancels the negative camber gained through compression on the outer wheel).

**Ackermann Geometry**
Ackermann geometry determines how much the inner front wheel turns relative to the outer front wheel during steering. At low speed with negligible slip angles, 100% Ackermann (geometric Ackermann) is correct — the inner wheel needs to turn more sharply because it traces a tighter arc. At higher speeds where significant slip angles develop, both tyres need similar slip angles to generate proportional force, which means reduced or even anti-Ackermann geometry is appropriate — the inner wheel turns less than geometric Ackermann would dictate. AC models Ackermann geometry through the steering arm attachment points, and the degree of Ackermann (parallel, 100%, reduced, or anti-Ackermann) emerges from these coordinates.

**Bump Steer**
Bump steer is the change in toe angle that occurs as the suspension compresses or extends. It is modeled in AC and emerges from the position of the steering rack tie rod attachment points relative to the suspension geometry. Bump steer causes the wheels to steer themselves during suspension travel — over bumps, during roll, or under braking dive. The effect is visible through the AC developer apps as a toe-vs-travel curve. Bump steer is not adjustable through the setup screen but is an important factor in understanding why some cars feel more stable or more nervous over bumps than others despite similar alignment settings.

## Adjustable Parameters and Effects

**Front camber** is adjustable on virtually all circuit cars, typically via shims, eccentric bolts, or adjustment slots on the upper or lower control arm. Increasing negative front camber improves front cornering grip by keeping the contact patch flat against the road during cornering load. It also increases inner tyre temperature and wear. Insufficient negative camber leaves grip on the table: the outer shoulder of the tyre does most of the work while the inner section runs cool and underloaded. Excessive negative camber reduces straight-line braking grip and causes rapid inner shoulder wear.

**Rear camber** serves the same contact patch optimisation role for the rear tyres. Rear camber directly influences oversteer and understeer balance: more negative rear camber generally increases rear cornering grip and reduces oversteer tendency. Because the rear axle does not steer, camber effects on the rear are more uniform across a lap; rear tyre temperature distribution is therefore a cleaner diagnostic signal than the front, which is influenced by both camber and the steering angle variations through each corner.

**Front toe** has the strongest effect on turn-in character and straight-line stability. Toe-out on the front makes the car respond more quickly to initial steering inputs but can make it feel nervous on corner exit and under braking. Toe-in on the front gives a more stable, predictable feel at the expense of a slower, more sluggish initial response. Front toe is one of the most sensitive alignment parameters: even small changes (fractions of a millimetre at the contact patch) produce noticeable driver feedback changes.

**Rear toe** is primarily a stability parameter. Toe-in on the rear is the standard configuration for rear-wheel-drive and front-wheel-drive cars alike; it resists rear-end rotation under lateral loading and makes the car feel planted and predictable. Toe-out on the rear is rarely used in circuit applications because it makes the rear axle over-responsive to yaw disturbances, producing instability or snap oversteer. Rear toe-in can be increased to cure mild oversteer or to improve confidence under trailing-throttle braking into corners.

**Toe Trade-Off Specifics**
Front toe-out provides faster turn-in response because the outer front tyre is already at a small slip angle toward the corner when steering begins, pre-loading the contact patch. However, front toe-out reduces straight-line stability — the car may feel nervous at high speed and under braking. The sensitivity is high: even 0.5 mm of total toe change produces noticeable driver feedback differences. Rear toe-in is universally used in competition, typically in the range of 0.5 to 1.5 mm total (0.25 to 0.75 mm per side). Rear toe-in creates self-correcting yaw forces that stabilize the rear axle — any yaw disturbance causes the rear tyres to generate opposing forces that resist rotation. Increasing rear toe-in beyond the optimal range introduces excessive drag from constant rear tyre scrub, which costs straight-line speed and accelerates rear tyre wear.

**Caster** (where adjustable) affects steering feel weight, self-centering speed, and the front camber change during steering. More caster produces better camber recovery during cornering (beneficial at slow-speed corners with high steering angles) and heavier, more communicative steering. Less caster lightens the steering and reduces self-centering, which can be useful at circuits where very fast transitions require lighter steering inputs. Caster adjustment is less common than camber and toe; many cars fix it via prescribed shim positions or geometry constraints.

## Telemetry Diagnosis

**Tyre temperature inner/mid/outer distribution** is the primary telemetry tool for evaluating camber. A well-set camber produces approximately even temperatures across the three measurement points, with the mid zone running slightly hotter than both edges due to tyre construction. If the inner temperature significantly exceeds the mid and outer temperatures, the wheel has too much negative camber: the inner shoulder is overloaded. If the outer temperature is highest, the wheel has too little negative camber: the tyre is rolling onto its outer edge under cornering load. As a rough guide, an inner-to-outer spread greater than 15-20 degrees Celsius indicates a camber correction is warranted.

**Temperature differences between front left and front right** (or rear left and rear right) after multiple laps can indicate that static camber settings are asymmetric relative to the track's corner distribution, or that the setup is generating imbalanced loading. Tracks with a predominance of right-hand corners may naturally run the left-front tyre hotter; this is a circuit characteristic, not necessarily a setup error.

**Tyre wear patterns** complement temperature readings. A worn inner shoulder with normal outer and mid wear confirms excessive negative camber. Accelerated wear across the full contact patch with even temperature distribution is typically a pressure issue rather than a camber issue. Feathering (sawtooth wear pattern visible on tread blocks) is a classic symptom of toe errors: each block is being dragged sideways on contact, causing one edge of each block to wear faster than the other.

**Slip angle behaviour** provides indirect alignment information. If telemetry shows the front slip angles are consistently high even at moderate lateral acceleration — indicating the front tyre is always working near its limit — this may point to insufficient front camber or excessive front toe-in causing slow initial load build-up. Rear slip angles that grow rapidly and then stabilise suggest the rear alignment is promoting a stable yaw response; rear slip angles that continue growing after lateral G plateaus suggest the rear is not generating sufficient self-correcting forces, which can indicate insufficient rear toe-in.

**Steering angle vs lateral G correlation** is a useful macro-level alignment indicator. For a given lateral G value, a well-aligned car should require a consistent and proportional steering angle. If the driver requires unusually large steering angles to achieve moderate lateral G, the front alignment may be causing high friction scrub or the toe setting is fighting the natural cornering geometry. If steering angle is small but lateral G is also low, understeer may be caused by insufficient front camber or excessive toe-in damping the initial response.

**Thermal Gradient as Camber Diagnostic**
The inner-to-outer temperature gradient across the tyre surface is the primary camber diagnostic. Under sustained cornering load, a well-set camber produces the outer tyre's inner zone approximately 5–10 degrees Celsius hotter than the outer zone, with the mid zone in between. This temperature differential reflects the contact patch loading distribution: negative camber shifts loading toward the inner shoulder, which heats more under cornering. A larger gradient (inner much hotter than outer) indicates excessive negative camber; a reversed gradient (outer hotter than inner) indicates insufficient negative camber for the cornering loads experienced. This diagnostic is most reliable from data collected during sustained high-lateral-G corners rather than short chicanes or mixed-direction sequences.

**Steering Angle vs Lateral G Scatter Plot for Ackermann Effects**
Plotting steering angle against lateral G for all data points across a lap produces a scatter cloud whose shape reveals Ackermann geometry effects. With 100% Ackermann, the cloud tends to show a steeper slope (more steering angle per unit of lateral G) at low speeds and a shallower slope at high speeds, because the inner wheel turns more than needed at higher slip angles. Anti-Ackermann produces the opposite pattern. The scatter plot also reveals asymmetries between left and right turns that may indicate alignment issues, asymmetric steering geometry, or a circuit with predominantly one-direction corners. This diagnostic requires multiple corners at different speeds to be informative.
