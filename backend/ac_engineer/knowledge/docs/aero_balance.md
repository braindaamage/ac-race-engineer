# Aero Balance

## Physical Principles

Aerodynamic downforce is generated when air moving over and under the car creates a pressure differential that pushes the car toward the track surface. The primary sources of downforce are wings (front and rear), the diffuser (underfloor), and the car body itself. Wings operate by accelerating airflow over a curved upper surface, reducing pressure above the wing relative to below it; the resulting net force acts downward on the car. The diffuser channels air from beneath the car and expands it rapidly at the rear, lowering underbody pressure and producing suction-based downforce — often the dominant source on purpose-built racing machinery with a flat floor.

Every downforce-generating element also produces aerodynamic drag, the resistive force opposing forward motion. The drag-downforce trade-off is fundamental: steeper wing angles and more aggressive diffuser geometries yield higher downforce but significantly increase drag, reducing straight-line speed. The efficiency of an aero configuration is often expressed as lift-to-drag ratio (L/D); maximizing this ratio is the primary objective of aerodynamic optimization.

Front-to-rear aero balance determines how downforce is distributed between the front and rear axles. Because downforce adds to the normal load on each axle, it directly increases the lateral grip available from those tyres. A car with more downforce at the front will tend toward understeer at high speeds; more rear downforce increases oversteer tendency at high speeds. Critically, because aerodynamic forces scale with the square of velocity, aero balance effects become dominant at high speed and nearly negligible at low speed. This creates a fundamental challenge: a setup optimized for high-speed aero balance may feel neutral at speed but exhibit different characteristics in slow corners where mechanical grip dominates.

Ground effect refers to the enhancement of underfloor downforce as ride height decreases. At lower ride heights, the gap between the floor and the track surface narrows, accelerating underbody airflow and increasing the pressure differential. This relationship is non-linear and car-specific: most cars have an optimal ride height range where downforce peaks, below which the floor stalls and downforce collapses suddenly. The aero map describes the full three-dimensional relationship between downforce (and drag), ride height (front and rear independently), and vehicle speed. Understanding the aero map is essential for predicting how suspension settings, fuel load changes, and tyre wear will alter aerodynamic behaviour over a race stint.

## Adjustable Parameters and Effects

**Front wing angle and flap**: Increasing the front wing angle steepens the angle of attack of the wing element relative to the airflow, generating more downforce at the front axle. This increases front grip at high speed, reducing high-speed understeer and improving turn-in. The cost is increased front drag and a forward shift in the aero balance that can introduce high-speed oversteer if taken too far. Some cars feature a two-element front wing where the main plane and an adjustable flap can be set independently, allowing fine-tuning of downforce level and stall characteristics.

**Rear wing angle**: Rear wing adjustment functions analogously to the front wing but affects the rear axle. Higher rear wing angles add rear downforce, stabilizing the car under high-speed braking and through fast corners, at the expense of top speed. On circuits with long straights, a lower rear wing setting sacrifices some cornering stability for significant straight-line speed gains. The rear wing is typically the single largest contributor to total drag in the aero package.

**Splitter height and front diffuser**: The front splitter manages airflow entering beneath the car and can be adjusted for height in some configurations. A lower splitter increases the velocity of air entering the underfloor, enhancing diffuser effectiveness. It also affects front-end ride height sensitivity.

**Diffuser settings**: On cars with adjustable diffuser geometry, angle and height affect the expansion rate of underfloor airflow at the rear. A more aggressive diffuser angle increases rear downforce but is sensitive to ride height variation and can stall if the rear drops too low under load.

**Ride height effect on aero**: Because of ground effect, ride height is both a suspension and aerodynamic adjustment. Lowering the front increases underbody airflow velocity and typically adds front downforce, shifting aero balance forward. Lowering the rear increases rear downforce. However, changes in ride height also affect the mechanical balance, pitch stiffness, and the car's sensitivity to bump-induced ride height variation, so aero and mechanical effects must be considered together.

**Gurney flaps**: A Gurney flap is a small vertical tab mounted at the trailing edge of a wing element, typically perpendicular to the chord. Despite its small size, it creates a low-pressure recirculation zone behind the wing that effectively increases the wing's camber, generating more downforce with a modest drag penalty. Gurney flaps are a useful tool for fine-tuning downforce levels when wing angle adjustments are too coarse, and they affect balance shift more subtly than full angle changes.

**Top speed, cornering grip, and balance at different speeds**: Reducing total wing angle improves top speed at the cost of cornering grip throughout the speed range. Because aero forces scale with velocity squared, these trade-offs are most pronounced at high-speed venues. On slower or more technical circuits, mechanical grip dominates and aero level matters less for overall lap time, making a lower-drag configuration viable even if it slightly compromises fast corner performance.

## Telemetry Diagnosis

**Speed-dependent balance shift identification**: Aero-induced balance issues are distinguished from mechanical balance problems by their speed dependence. If a driver reports understeer that appears or worsens progressively as corner entry speed increases, and the same corner exhibits neutral or oversteering behaviour at low speed, this is a strong indicator of insufficient front downforce relative to the rear, or excessive rear downforce. Conversely, high-speed oversteer that disappears in slow corners points to a rear aero deficit. Plotting lateral acceleration versus speed for a series of corners of varying velocities can reveal systematic balance shifts consistent with aerodynamic origin.

**High-speed vs low-speed cornering grip comparison**: Comparing lateral g levels achieved in fast corners versus slow corners of similar geometric radius provides a basis for separating aero grip from mechanical grip. If lateral acceleration in fast corners is disproportionately low relative to slow-corner performance, total downforce may be insufficient. If the ratio of fast-corner to slow-corner lateral g differs significantly between front and rear axles (inferred from oversteer/understeer feedback), the front-to-rear aero balance requires adjustment.

**Drag analysis from speed traces on straights**: Aerodynamic drag can be estimated from straight-line deceleration rate in coasting sections (throttle off, no brakes) or from acceleration rate through a given speed range. Higher drag manifests as slower acceleration through the speed band where the engine is power-limited, and higher terminal deceleration in coasting. Comparing speed traces at the same circuit with different wing configurations isolates the drag contribution of wing changes. Top speed at the end of the longest straight is a practical proxy for total drag level; changes of 1-3 km/h per wing step are typical, depending on the car.

**Ride height variation under aero load**: At high speed, aerodynamic downforce compresses the suspension, reducing ride height relative to the static setup measurement. This dynamic ride height reduction is proportional to speed squared and inversely proportional to spring and ride height stiffness. If data logging includes suspension displacement or ride height channels, the minimum ride height seen at peak speed on a straight should be checked against the aero map's stall threshold. Cars running very low static ride heights may enter a stall region at high speed, causing a sudden loss of downforce that is not always apparent to the driver but is visible as an unexpected reduction in lateral g or as instability on high-speed corners preceded by a long straight. Monitoring the difference between static and minimum dynamic ride height across a session also reveals changes attributable to fuel load reduction and tyre wear.
