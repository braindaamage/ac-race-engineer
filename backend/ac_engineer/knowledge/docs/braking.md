# Braking

## Physical Principles

Braking performance is governed by the friction force the tyres can generate against the road surface and the efficiency with which that force is distributed across all four wheels. When a driver applies the brakes, hydraulic pressure actuates the brake calipers at each corner, clamping the pads against the rotors and converting kinetic energy into heat. The total deceleration available is bounded by the tyre's peak longitudinal grip, which is itself affected by vertical load, surface temperature, and compound characteristics.

**Longitudinal weight transfer** occurs during every braking event. As the car decelerates, inertia shifts mass forward, increasing the normal load on the front axle and reducing it at the rear. This transfer is proportional to deceleration magnitude, the vehicle's centre-of-gravity height, and its wheelbase. A heavier forward load increases the grip capacity of the front tyres, while the unloaded rear becomes more susceptible to locking. The magnitude of this transfer must be accounted for in brake balance calibration.

**Brake bias** (also called brake balance) describes the front-to-rear distribution of braking force. Because the front tyres carry more load during braking, they can sustain a higher braking force before locking. Setting bias too far rearward causes premature rear lock-up, inducing oversteer instability. Setting it too far forward produces front lock-up, reducing steering authority and extending stopping distances.

**Engine braking** contributes additional deceleration at the driven axle when the driver lifts off the throttle. In rear-wheel-drive and four-wheel-drive configurations, this acts predominantly on the rear axle, adding to the effective brake force there. If the engine braking contribution is large, the rear axle can reach its grip limit before the hydraulic brakes would alone, effectively shifting the balance rearward. This interaction must be considered when setting brake bias on downshifts.

**Brake temperature** has a decisive influence on performance and fade. Brake components — pads, rotors, and calipers — operate within a defined thermal window. Below the minimum operating temperature, friction coefficient is reduced and braking feel is inconsistent. Above the maximum threshold, the pad material degrades, gases form at the pad-rotor interface, and friction coefficient drops sharply — a condition known as brake fade. Sustained high temperatures can also cause rotor cracking and fluid vaporisation (vapour lock), resulting in sudden loss of pedal pressure.

**Trail braking** is the technique of carrying brake pressure into the corner entry phase, overlapping it with initial steering input. As brake pressure is progressively released while steering angle increases, the weight that was transferred forward under braking remains biased toward the front axle slightly longer, increasing front grip and rotation potential. This allows the car to turn in more sharply and delays the point at which the rear must accept full lateral load. The technique requires precise modulation because simultaneous longitudinal and lateral demands share the tyre's total friction budget (the friction circle).

**ABS interaction**: In vehicles equipped with ABS, the system monitors individual wheel deceleration rates and modulates hydraulic pressure to prevent full lock-up. This effectively acts as a safety floor but does not replace accurate bias setting — a poorly balanced bias will cause one axle to repeatedly trigger ABS intervention before the other, reducing total braking efficiency and introducing handling imbalance.

## Adjustable Parameters and Effects

**Brake bias percentage** is the primary tuning lever for brake balance, expressed as the proportion of total braking force sent to the front axle (e.g., 58% front / 42% rear). Increasing front bias improves rear stability under heavy braking but risks front lock-up. Reducing front bias frees up the fronts under braking but can induce rear instability, particularly at high speed or on low-grip surfaces. Optimal bias varies by track (corner distribution, braking zones, surface grip), tyre condition, fuel load, and aerodynamic configuration. Many cars allow in-cockpit adjustment so the driver can shift bias lap by lap as fuel burns or tyres degrade.

**Brake duct size (cooling)** controls the volume of ambient air directed at the brake assembly. Larger ducts or wider duct openings increase airflow and lower peak operating temperatures. Smaller duct openings retain heat, helping brakes reach operating temperature quickly in cold conditions or at tracks with few heavy braking zones. The correct duct setting balances keeping the brakes within their thermal window without overcooling them on circuits where sustained temperatures are needed for consistent pad bite. Overcooling causes the same inconsistency as undercooling — the pad never fully activates and braking feel varies corner to corner.

**Pad compound selection** determines the friction coefficient, thermal tolerance, and response characteristics of the brake system. Harder compounds withstand higher temperatures but deliver lower friction coefficients at low temperatures and require a longer warm-up phase. Softer compounds offer higher peak friction and faster warm-up but degrade more rapidly under sustained heat. Compound choice interacts directly with duct sizing: a soft compound at a circuit requiring large ducts may never reach operating temperature; a hard compound with small ducts on an endurance circuit may overheat progressively.

**Engine brake map** (where adjustable) controls the magnitude of engine braking torque at the driven wheels during deceleration. Higher engine brake settings produce stronger deceleration on lift-off but impose additional longitudinal load on the driven axle, which reduces available lateral grip during combined braking-and-turning phases. Lower engine brake settings give a smoother, more predictable deceleration profile and preserve more rear lateral grip for corner entry but may require more hydraulic brake pressure to achieve the same stopping distances.

**Brake pressure sensitivity** (or brake pedal mapping) adjusts how aggressively hydraulic pressure builds relative to pedal travel or pedal force input. A more progressive mapping allows finer modulation of brake pressure, which aids trail braking technique and reduces the risk of sudden lock-up. A more aggressive mapping delivers peak pressure rapidly, which can help in cars with a long pedal travel or drivers who prefer high-initial-bite feel. On circuits where braking zones are short and sharp, a responsive mapping reduces reaction distance losses; on circuits with long braking zones requiring sustained partial braking, a progressive mapping helps maintain consistency.

## Telemetry Diagnosis

**Brake trace analysis** examines the recorded brake pressure or brake application channel against time or distance. A well-executed braking event shows a rapid initial pressure peak (threshold braking), a sustained plateau at near-maximum deceleration, and a progressive trail-off as the car decelerates and corner entry begins. An irregular or spiking trace indicates inconsistent application — either technique variation or a mechanical issue such as brake fluid vaporisation. A trace that plateaus far below expected maximum pressure suggests the driver is not reaching threshold braking, leaving deceleration performance on the table.

**Lock-up detection from wheel speed differential** compares individual wheel speed channels during a braking event. Under normal braking, all four wheels decelerate together in proportion to their braking load. When a wheel locks, its speed drops sharply — often to zero — while the vehicle continues at a higher speed. This produces a clear divergence between the locked wheel's speed signal and the others. The axle on which the lock-up occurs identifies whether bias is too far forward (front lock-up) or too far rearward (rear lock-up). Frequency and duration of lock-up events can be correlated with track position to identify specific braking zones requiring bias adjustment.

**Brake temperature monitoring** uses thermal sensor channels — where available in the data — to track peak and average brake temperatures at each corner. Comparing temperatures across a stint reveals whether the brakes are operating within their optimal window throughout. Progressively increasing temperatures across consecutive laps at the same braking zones indicate insufficient cooling relative to heat input. Asymmetric temperatures between left and right brakes at the same axle may indicate a sticking caliper, uneven pad wear, or differential duct airflow. Unusually low temperatures suggest overcooling or that the driver is braking well below maximum.

**Deceleration G-force patterns** derived from the longitudinal acceleration channel reveal braking efficiency and consistency. Plotting peak deceleration values for each braking zone across multiple laps allows comparison against the theoretical grip-limited maximum. Large lap-to-lap variance in the same braking zone indicates technique inconsistency or mechanical issues such as fade. A systematic reduction in peak deceleration late in a stint can indicate brake fade as temperatures exceed the pad's operating limit, or tyre degradation reducing the friction available to support braking force.

**Trail braking identification from overlapping brake and steering inputs** is performed by plotting the brake pressure channel and the steering angle channel on the same time axis. When both channels are simultaneously non-zero — brake pressure declining while steering angle increases — the driver is executing trail braking into corner entry. The overlap duration and the rate of brake pressure release during this phase reveal technique detail: a long, controlled overlap indicates deliberate trail braking, while abrupt brake release before steering input suggests a lift-and-turn approach. Cross-referencing this with lateral G-force and minimum corner speed data allows quantification of whether trail braking is increasing corner entry speed at a given turn.
